Weather

Taiga is characterized by very cold weather, low rate of precipitation. (Snow and rain) and a short growing season. A long and intense winter lasting up to 6 months,with average temperatures below freezing. Summer is short and long, maybe 50 to 100 days without frost. The winters in the taiga are long and cold.

Seasons

The main seasons of the taiga are winter and summer. Spring and fall are so short that you hardly know they exist. Taiga is either hot, humid or very cold. There are few taiga plants due to the harsh environment.

Climate

The taiga climate is for the most part dominated by cold arctic air. Exceptionally cold winds bring bitterly cold air from the Arctic Circle: the temperatures fall even more on clear nights when there is no cloud cover. Because of earth's tilt, the taiga is turned away from the sun in the winter. Less of the sun's radiation reaches the ground to warm it up.

Elevation and Geology

So at around 3,000 feet in elevation, more or less, the predominant biome becomes taiga, which is more commonly known as coniferous forest or a forest composed of evergreen conifers. and also The taiga is a forest of the cold, subarctic region. The subarctic is an area of the Northern Hemisphere that lies just south of the Arctic Circle. Taiga lies between the tundra to the north and temperate forests to the south. Alaska, Canada, Scandinavia, and Siberia have taigas.

Fauna

Most animals migrate to warmer climates when the weather starts to get cold. Some animals adapted to life in the taiga by hibernating as the temperature dropped. Other animals have adapted to very cold temperatures by producing insulated fur or wool to protect them from the cold. In some cases, seasonal adjustments to the fur or coat will protect the animal from predators. Ermine, a small mammal, is a good example of this adaptation. Dark brown summer coat will turn white in winter. This adjustment allows Ermin to blend in with his surroundings and makes it harder for Ermin's hunters to see.

Northern pike

Northern pike are circumpolar in the freshwater world of the northern hemisphere. Adult northern pike are usually solitary and highly territorial. Spawning adults will begin to move inshore or upstream to marsh areas to spawn as soon as the ice begins to break up or thaw in early spring. Spawning will normally occur during daylight hours in shallow quiet areas with a weed bottom.

Wood frog

Wood frogs have adapted to cold climates by freezing them in winter. During this time, they stop breathing and their heart stops beating. Their bodies produce special antifreeze that prevents ice from freezing inside the cells, which can be life-threatening. Ice formed in the spaces between the cells. As the weather warms up, the frogs will melt and begin feeding and mating again.

Red sided garter snake

The red-sided gartersnake is a habitat generalist and, during its active season, occupies habitat similar to that of the eastern gartersnake – forests, shrublands, wetlands, fields and rocky areas. High-quality hibernation sites that are deep and below the frost line are crucial for this snake, because its range reaches further north than that of any other land-dwelling reptile in Canada.

Brown bear

Brown bears live mostly in the taiga. There are three subspecies In North America they are grizzly and Kodiak bears, and in Europe and Asia, they are Eurasian brown bears. This bear has many useful modifications to help it survive in the taiga. First, the bear hibernates to survive the bitter winter. The second is general and omnivore. This allows the bear to find plenty of food because there is a wide selection of them. Brown bears have a very good smell to help them find food.

Flora

How did trees adapt?

  • Conifers have adapted to survive the long, cold winters and short summers of the taiga.
  • Their needles contain very little sap, which helps prevent freezing.
  • The waxy coating on the tree needles prevents evaporation
  • The darkness of the needles helps to attract more sun
  • The roots of the confers will grow downwards which protects the trees from the winds that can be harsh at times in the Taiga’s winters.

Invasive species

And another threat to the taiga is invasive species. Invasive species such as the Emerald Ash Borer Beetle pose a significant threat to the taiga. These beetles destroy vast forests that is unaccustomed to surviving.

Deforestaion

One of the main environmental problems for taiga is deforestation or tree cutting. Unfortunately, many of the products we use every day are from logging or tree harvesting for human use in the taiga. Most of the wood that complements our homes, such as the crossbeams and our hardwood floors, are from taiga. Paper products, such as newsprint, originate from trees in the taiga.

Acid Rain

Acid rain also posed a major problem for taiga forests. Acid rain is rain that is more acidic than normal. Scientists have discovered that air pollution from the burning of fossil fuels is the leading cause of acid rain. Power plants and coal and oil burning plants Smoke and smoke from the combustion of fossil fuels rise into the atmosphere and combine with air humidity to form acid rain. The main chemicals in air pollution that generate acid rain are sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides. Acid rain doesn't usually kill plants directly. But it tends to weaken trees by destroying their leaves, limiting available nutrients, or poisoning them with toxins that are slowly released from the soil. When trees are weak, they can be more easily attacked by the disease or insect that eventually kills them. Weak trees may be more easily injured by cold weather.

Extinction

Due to dangerous human activities such as hunting, we have brought many taiga animals near danger and extinction. The acquisition of valuable items such as fur, fashion and fresh meat from taiga, although hunting and slaughtering, the population of many species is not expected to be adjusted according to the original statistics.

Solution

Although humans will certainly destroy the environment But there are still solutions to the influence that humans have on the environment. Deforestation is the process of cutting down trees. Reforestation is the process of replanting or replacing trees that have been cut down or burned by fire. Planting new trees will help restore the natural balance of the ecosystem. Solving acid rain is easy. But not as easy as going out and planting a tree. Acid rain caused by pollution If humans reduce the amount of pollution that enters the atmosphere, it can reduce the amount of acid rain generated in the forest. Finding alternative energy sources and having a cleaner car are other ways to reduce the chance of acid rain.
